# Instant-Dissolving Creatine Chemistry

> Research memo for developing a compact creatine format that rapidly dissolves in hot water first, then hot coffee.

**Created**: 2026-05-18
**Owner**: R&D
**Status**: Research synthesis, prototype design pending

## Executive Finding

The active ingredient should remain **creatine monohydrate** for launch unless a supplier or legal review forces a change. It has the strongest human evidence base, highest regulatory comfort, and high active-creatine loading by mass. Other creatine salts can dissolve faster, but they generally contain less creatine per gram, rely on lower pH for solubility, and have weaker evidence that they improve muscle uptake or outcomes versus monohydrate.

The "instant cube" challenge is therefore not mainly about creating new covalent bonds in creatine. It is a **solid-state and reconstitution engineering problem**:

1. Increase water access into the compact.
2. Prevent hydrophobic/gel-like surface skin and floating clumps.
3. Keep particles small enough to dissolve quickly after release.
4. Use a food-grade matrix that breaks apart immediately in hot water.
5. Avoid acidic shelf-stable liquids and avoid long hold times in hot acidic drinks.

## Proven Chemistry

### Creatine Monohydrate

Creatine monohydrate is creatine with one molecule of water of crystallization. It crystallizes from water and contains about **87.9% creatine by mass**. The monohydrate powder is stable in dry form for years, including at elevated storage temperatures, but creatine becomes less stable once dissolved in water.

Key implications:

- Dry cube or sachet formats are chemically safer than ready-to-drink formats.
- Dissolve immediately before drinking.
- Shelf-life work should focus on moisture control, water activity, and packaging.

### Solubility Is Temperature-Dependent

Creatine monohydrate has limited solubility at room temperature, but heat helps dramatically:

| Temperature | Approximate creatine solubility in water |
|---:|---:|
| 4°C | 6 g/L |
| 20°C | 14 g/L |
| 50°C | 34 g/L |
| 60°C | 45 g/L |

For a 3 g dose, even 60°C water theoretically needs about 67 mL to fully dissolve the active. A 5 g dose theoretically needs about 111 mL at 60°C. Coffee volumes are usually sufficient, so the failure mode is more likely **slow wetting/dispersal** than absolute solubility.

### Stability In Solution

Creatine degrades into creatinine through intramolecular cyclization in aqueous solution. Rate increases with:

- lower pH,
- higher temperature,
- longer time in solution.

Literature summaries report strong dry stability but solution degradation over days at acidic pH. At 25°C after 3 days, degradation has been reported around 4% at pH 5.5, 12% at pH 4.5, and 21% at pH 3.5. This supports the FUSE strategy: **do not sell creatine in a shelf-stable acidic drink; sell a dry format that dissolves at use.**

For hot-water and coffee testing, the relevant hold time is minutes, not days. The target should be:

- dissolve in <=3 seconds,
- drink within 10-15 minutes,
- verify >95% creatine retained at 15 minutes by HPLC.

## Creatine Form Comparison

| Form | Dissolution advantage | Evidence / launch risk | Practical conclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creatine monohydrate | Low at room temp, much better in hot water | Best studied, accepted form, high active loading | Preferred active |
| Micronized creatine monohydrate | Faster apparent dissolution due to smaller particles | Same active/evidence as monohydrate | Strong candidate for core active |
| Creatine citrate / pyruvate | More soluble because lower pH / salt behavior | Less active creatine per gram; no clear superiority in muscle retention | Possible secondary test, not launch default |
| Creatine HCl | Much higher solubility in small water volumes | Less direct outcome evidence; lower active loading; acidic taste/pH concerns | Only consider if monohydrate UX cannot be solved |
| Creatine ethyl ester | Marketed as soluble | Stability concerns; can convert to creatinine faster | Do not prioritize |
| Liquid creatine | Convenient | Shelf-stability and dose challenges | Avoid for launch |

## What Bonds Or Interactions Matter

The formulation should avoid chemically modifying creatine unless Legal and R&D explicitly approve it. The useful interactions are mostly non-covalent or excipient-driven:

- **Ionic salt formation**: creatine can form salts with strong acids through protonation, especially around the guanidine moiety. This can improve solubility but changes pH, taste, active loading, and regulatory/evidence posture.
- **Hydrogen bonding / crystal water**: relevant to monohydrate crystal stability, not enough to create instant dissolution by itself.
- **Cyclodextrin inclusion / complexation**: non-covalent complexing can improve wetting/taste masking, but cost and manufacturing complexity are higher.
- **Hydrophilic matrix binding**: maltodextrin, modified starch, gum arabic, inulin, or similar carriers can create a water-friendly solid matrix.
- **Surfactant-assisted wetting**: small amounts of food-grade lecithin or another approved wetting agent can reduce surface tension and help liquid penetrate particles.
- **Effervescent acid-base reaction**: citric/tartaric acid plus bicarbonate releases CO2 and breaks a compact apart rapidly. This is powerful for hot water, but it may affect coffee taste, foam, sodium load, and pH.
- **Superdisintegrant swelling/wicking**: food/pharma excipients such as croscarmellose sodium or sodium starch glycolate rapidly pull water into a compact. Suitability for a UK food supplement format needs regulatory review.

## Cube Design Principles

An instant cube should be designed to **disintegrate first**, then dissolve. A dense hard cube of creatine monohydrate alone is likely to fail because the outside hydrates slowly and shields the interior.

Target physical properties:

- high porosity,
- low compression force,
- hydrophilic binder,
- fast wicking channels,
- micronized creatine particles,
- rapid breakup into small fragments,
- moisture-barrier packaging.

Avoid:

- dense hard compacts,
- high fat or wax coatings for the first prototype,
- gummy binders that form a surface film,
- acidic liquid pre-dissolved products,
- relying on "more solubility" claims from novel creatine forms without evidence.

## Prototype Routes

### Route A: Porous Agglomerated Monohydrate Cube

**Best first route for FUSE.**

Concept:

- micronized creatine monohydrate,
- maltodextrin or modified starch as hydrophilic matrix,
- tiny amount of lecithin or approved wetting agent,
- low-compression porous cube,
- optional freeze-dried or foam-dried structure if manufacturing supports it.

Hypothesis:

The cube collapses on contact with 75-90°C water, releases fine creatine particles, and dissolves without grittiness in <=3 seconds.

### Route B: Mild Effervescent Monohydrate Cube

Concept:

- creatine monohydrate,
- bicarbonate plus food acid,
- soluble binder,
- low residual moisture packaging.

Hypothesis:

CO2 generation forces breakup and dispersion quickly. Best for hot water tests. Coffee risk is sensory: fizz, foam, acidity changes, and taste drift.

### Route C: Cyclodextrin / Carrier Complex

Concept:

- creatine monohydrate dispersed or complexed with beta-cyclodextrin or a permitted cyclodextrin,
- spray-dried into instant granules,
- compressed lightly into a porous cube.

Hypothesis:

Improves wetting and taste neutrality, but cost and supplier complexity are higher.

### Route D: Creatine Salt Blend

Concept:

- small fraction creatine citrate, pyruvate, or HCl blended with monohydrate to improve initial wetting.

Hypothesis:

Could increase dissolution speed, but it dilutes monohydrate positioning and may introduce acidity/taste issues. Treat as fallback, not the first route.

## Hot-Water Testing Before Coffee

### Test Medium

Start with 200 mL deionized or low-mineral water at:

- 60°C,
- 75°C,
- 85°C,
- 95°C.

Then repeat in hard water to identify mineral effects before moving to coffee.

### Test Variables

| Variable | Levels |
|---|---|
| Dose | 3 g, 5 g |
| Format | loose powder, agglomerate, porous cube, effervescent cube |
| Particle size | standard, micronized |
| Matrix | maltodextrin, modified starch, inulin |
| Wetting aid | none, lecithin low dose |
| Compression | low, medium |

### Pass / Fail

Pass:

- visual disappearance <=3 seconds,
- no surface raft,
- no visible sediment at 30 seconds,
- no detectable grit in controlled sensory screen,
- pH stays acceptable for coffee transition,
- HPLC confirms >95% creatine retained after 15 minutes at target temperature.

Fail:

- clumping,
- floating film,
- sticky surface skin,
- visible sediment,
- taste-active excipient level,
- unacceptable foam or alkalinity.

## Recommendation

Move forward with **micronized creatine monohydrate in a porous hydrophilic agglomerate/cube**, not a chemically altered creatine, as the lead route. Use hot water as the first proof medium, because it isolates dissolution physics from coffee acids, oils, tannins, and suspended solids.

Run three prototype families:

1. **A1**: micronized monohydrate + maltodextrin/modified starch, low-compression cube.
2. **A2**: A1 plus trace lecithin or approved wetting aid.
3. **B1**: mild effervescent monohydrate cube as a benchmark for fastest possible breakup.

Only test creatine HCl/citrate blends if A1/A2 cannot meet the <=3 second target.
